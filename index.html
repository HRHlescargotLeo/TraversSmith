Worker status reform: what the Employment Rights Bill means in practice

Sarah Mitchell · Partner, Employment
14 May 2026 · 10 min read

The Employment Rights Bill received Royal Assent in April 2026, introducing the most significant reforms to employment law in a generation. This briefing sets out the key changes and what they mean for employers.

Background

The Bill consolidates and extends worker protections across several dimensions: the elimination of the three-tier worker status framework, enhanced unfair dismissal protections from day one of employment, and significant changes to zero-hours contract regulation.

Key changes

Two-tier status: The distinction between "worker" and "employee" is abolished for most purposes. A new single "worker" category will apply, with rights scaling by length of service.
Day-one unfair dismissal: The two-year qualifying period is removed. Employers will need robust onboarding and performance management frameworks from the point of hire.
Zero-hours contracts: Workers on zero-hours arrangements with regular patterns of work will have a right to a guaranteed-hours contract after 12 weeks.
Collective redundancy: The 90-day consultation trigger is retained but the threshold for "establishment" is now assessed at group level.

What employers should do now

Most provisions come into force on 1 January 2027. Employers should begin a review of: (i) existing worker and contractor arrangements; (ii) probationary period and performance review processes; and (iii) the position of zero-hours workers who may acquire guaranteed-hours rights.
